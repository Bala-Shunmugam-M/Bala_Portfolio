Motor Energy Efficiency Audit
Industrial Facility

Leveraged B.E. EEE knowledge to audit 8 industrial motors in a manufacturing facility. Used clamp meter and power analyser measurements to identify load mismatches — 3 motors severely underloaded and 2 overloaded — and prescribed targeted VFD and right-sizing interventions with a 14-month payback.

Problem
High electricity bills with no clear explanation. Motors were sized based on peak load assumptions made during plant design — never re-evaluated against actual operating conditions. No one had measured actual motor loading since installation.
Methodology
Used a clamp meter and power analyser on each motor's supply cable during normal production hours. Measured: voltage (V), current (A), power factor (cos φ), and input kW. Calculated load factor = Actual kW / Rated kW for each motor.
Why EEE Background Matters
Understanding motor equivalent circuit, slip, magnetising current, and the relationship between load factor and efficiency allowed me to interpret the measurements correctly — knowing that a motor at 40% load has poor efficiency due to high reactive current, not just low real power.
Key Finding
3 of 8 motors were running below 40% load — the efficiency sweet spot is 75–100%. Below 40%, iron losses and magnetising current dominate, making efficiency worse than nameplate suggests. 2 motors were overloaded, risking insulation damage and premature failure.

Reading the data
Row highlighted in red = overloaded (load factor > 100%) — immediate risk. Row highlighted in blue = severely underloaded — candidates for right-sizing and VFD. Motors M4, M5, M8 are already operating efficiently and require no intervention.
The Affinity Laws — why VFDs save so much energy
For centrifugal machines (pumps, fans): Flow Speed. Pressure Speed². Power Speed³. A 20% reduction in speed = a 49% reduction in power consumed. This cube relationship is why VFDs produce dramatic energy savings even with modest speed reductions.
01
Throttling valve vs VFD: The facility was controlling flow by partially closing valves — like driving a car with the accelerator fully pressed while braking. The motor still runs at full speed, consuming full power, while the valve dissipates energy as heat. A VFD slows the motor instead — using only the power actually needed.
02
Soft-start benefit: VFDs also eliminate the inrush current spike at motor start (typically 6–8× rated current for a direct-on-line start). This extends motor and cable life and reduces electrical demand charges on the utility bill.
03
Power factor improvement: Active front-end VFD topologies can maintain near-unity power factor at the supply point — reducing reactive power demand and eliminating power factor penalty charges on the electricity tariff.
Priority 1
Replace M6 and M7 immediately — both are overloaded (110–112% load factor), risking winding insulation failure and unexpected breakdown. A motor burnout is a much larger cost than a planned replacement.
Priority 2
Right-size M2 and M3 and install VFDs — replace the oversized 30–37 kW motors with correctly sized 15 kW units. Add VFD to each. Projected saving: ~45 kW continuous reduction = ~110,000 kWh/year saved.
Priority 3
Install VFD on M1 (conveyor drive) — at 42% load factor, a VFD can reduce speed by 20–30% during partial-load periods, cutting power by 49–66% during those intervals.
Financial summary
Total projected reduction: ~20% of facility electricity consumption. Estimated VFD investment for M1–M3: ₹2.8–3.5 lakh. Annual electricity savings at ₹8/kWh: ₹2.8–3.2 lakh/year. Payback: ~14 months.
