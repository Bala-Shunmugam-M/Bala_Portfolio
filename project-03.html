Problem
Daily output of 487 units consistently below the 500-unit target, resulting in 83% overall efficiency against the 90% management benchmark. Root cause was unclear — multiple stages experienced downtime, making it difficult to identify the primary constraint without structured analysis.
Methodology
Walked all 5 production stages daily for the internship period. Logged start and end times of each stoppage event with reason codes. Aggregated by stage to compute average daily downtime and percentage contribution. Cross-validated against production log sheets.
Theory of Constraints Applied
Goldratt's Theory of Constraints states: the throughput of any system is determined by its weakest link (the constraint). All other stages have excess capacity relative to the constraint — so improving non-constraints doesn't improve system throughput. Focus all effort on the cooler.
Key Finding
The cooler stage contributed 1.1 out of 2.4 total downtime hours per day — 46% of system downtime despite being one of five stages. The next largest contributor was the kiln at just 0.5 hr/day. Eliminating cooler downtime alone would push efficiency from 83% to near 90%.

Why the cooler breaks down most often
The cooler handles the highest thermal stress in the production line — it receives hot material directly from the kiln and must cool it rapidly. Repeated thermal cycling degrades mechanical components faster than any other stage. Without predictive monitoring, failures arrive without warning.
01
Thermal fatigue: Repeated heating and cooling cycles cause micro-cracking in cooler grate plates and seals, leading to periodic breakdowns that appear random but follow a predictable cumulative damage pattern.
02
No early warning system: Operators had no temperature or vibration monitoring on cooler components. Failures were discovered reactively — only when the line stopped — rather than proactively from condition monitoring trends.
03
Cascading idle time: When the cooler stops, upstream stages (kiln, bunker, conveyor) must also stop or slow — amplifying the direct downtime into system-wide lost output far greater than the cooler downtime alone.
04
Week 3 spike to 76%: Traced to an unplanned cooler replacement event. The absence of a standby component and a slow procurement process extended the stoppage — a supply chain vulnerability compounding the maintenance gap.
Immediate
Install temperature monitoring on cooler grate plates — wireless sensors transmitting to the DCS. Set alarm thresholds before failure temperature to give operators 30–60 minutes warning for planned intervention.
Immediate
Maintain one spare grate assembly on-site — the most frequently replaced cooler component. Estimated cost < ₹50,000; eliminates the 8–12 hr procurement delay that extended the Week 3 stoppage.
Short-term
Establish a preventive replacement schedule for grate plates and seals based on cumulative operating hours — before thermal fatigue failure occurs. Align to planned weekend maintenance windows to avoid mid-shift breakdowns.
Short-term
Apply Theory of Constraints five-step process: 1) Identify — cooler confirmed. 2) Exploit — maximise cooler uptime. 3) Subordinate — adjust all other stages to support cooler. 4) Elevate — invest in cooler capacity and reliability. 5) Repeat — check for new constraint.
Medium-term
Projected outcome: Reducing cooler downtime from 1.1 to 0.3 hr/day (matching the kiln as the new limiting stage) would raise overall efficiency from 83% to approximately 90% — closing the performance gap entirely.
