The Peak Paradox
Tamil Nadu peak demand occurs in April–June when ambient temperatures are highest. Gas turbines are air-breathing: each 1°C above ISO 15°C baseline = 0.6% output loss. At +15°C ambient, the plant is already running 9% below nameplate when the grid needs it most.
Capacity Metrics
Key metrics for peaking plants: Equivalent Forced Outage Rate (EFOR) — percentage of time unavailable due to unplanned outage; Availability Factor — fraction of time able to generate; Capacity Factor — actual vs maximum possible output (low for peaking units, by design).
Operations Interventions
1. Inlet evaporative cooling — recovers 4–8% summer derating. 2. Steam/water injection — augments mass flow, boosts power during peaks. 3. Peak rating — elevated firing temperature at cost of shorter inspection intervals. 4. Zero planned outages April–June.
Financial Opportunity
Inlet cooling capital cost: ₹1.5–2.5 Cr. Additional summer revenue from recovered output: ₹1.1–1.6 Cr per summer season. Simple payback: 1.5–2.5 years. NPV positive at any reasonable discount rate given 20+ year plant life.

Fuel Supply Risk
Single-pipeline dependency: GAIL pipeline is the sole natural gas feed. Any infrastructure disruption causes immediate dispatch unavailability. No above-ground gas storage. 48-hour naphtha buffer stock is the only hedge.
Price Volatility
Natural gas prices linked to international LNG spot markets and long-term contracted rates. Unhedged fuel cost exposure impacts plant economics directly — fuel = 60–75% of variable cost. Wobbe Index variations in pipeline gas require combustor tuning adjustments.
Spare Parts Tension
Classic OM tension: holding costs (expensive slow-moving GT spares tie up capital) vs stockout costs (a missing Stage-1 bucket delays a 48,000 EOH major inspection). OEM lead times of 6–18 months make this especially acute for critical rotating parts.
Mitigation Strategy
Minimum 48-hour naphtha buffer + dual-fuel readiness protocol. Spare parts: ABC-classify all GT spares; maintain critical A-items in-house; use regional pool agreements for B-items; rely on OEM ordering for C-items.
