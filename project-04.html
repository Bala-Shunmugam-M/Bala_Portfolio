Impact
Problem
Two simultaneous failures: excess inventory tying up working capital in high holding costs, and periodic stockouts causing production stoppages. Neither problem was visible in isolation — the inventory appeared large enough, yet shortages still occurred due to poor reorder timing.
Analysis
Collected annual demand (D), ordering cost per purchase order (S), and holding cost per unit per year (H). Plotted total cost curve against varying order quantities to identify the minimum — the EOQ point where ordering cost and holding cost are exactly equal.
Insight — The Real Problem
The organisation was ordering in large infrequent batches to get supplier discounts — driving up holding cost while also creating long gaps between orders where stockouts occurred mid-cycle. Ordering more frequently in smaller batches costs more per order but saves far more in holding and stockout costs.
Solution Framework
Three-part solution: EOQ gives the optimal order quantity. ROP (Reorder Point = lead-time demand) tells you when to order. Safety Stock (= z × σ_demand × √lead_time) provides a buffer against demand variability — eliminating stockouts without excess inventory.
EOQ Formula Derivation
Total annual cost = Ordering cost + Holding cost = (D/Q)×S + (Q/2)×H. Minimise by differentiating with respect to Q and setting to zero: dTC/dQ = −DS/Q² + H/2 = 0 → Q* = √(2DS/H). At this point, ordering cost exactly equals holding cost — the economic optimum.

Key Takeaway
The EOQ model works not because it produces a magic number, but because it forces an organisation to precisely quantify the opposing cost forces — ordering and holding — and find the exact point where they balance. Most organisations over-order to avoid stockouts and unknowingly pay 2–3× the optimal holding cost as a result.
